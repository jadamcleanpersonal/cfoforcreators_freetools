Every creator eventually asks the same question: "how much should I charge for this?"

Most answers are useless. "It depends on your engagement." "Your rate should be X CPM." "Ask for 10% of your annual revenue per deal."

None of that actually helps you decide what to type in the rate field on a brand proposal.

This is the version that does.

---

## 1. How brands actually decide what to pay

Brands don't make up numbers. They have rate cards.

A rate card is an internal document that says "for a YouTube tech creator with 500k subscribers, we'll pay $3,500–5,500 for an integration." Every major brand with an influencer marketing budget has one. Most mid-market brands do too. And they update them annually, usually pulling from the same public sources we used to build this calculator: Karat's pricing guides, Influencer Marketing Hub reports, and their own deal history.

When you quote a rate, they're running it against their rate card — not negotiating from scratch. If you're in range, they say yes (or counteroffer slightly lower). If you're 2x over, they don't negotiate — they just don't reply.

This means two things:

**You can undercharge and not know it.** Brands will happily pay you below-market rates. They're not going to say "actually you're worth more." They'll close the deal and move on.

**You can overcharge and get silence.** Not "we can't do that," not "can we negotiate" — just nothing. Brands ghost overpriced creators constantly. If you've pitched 10 brands and heard nothing, your rate might be the problem.

The calculator exists to tell you which bucket you're in before you hit send.

## 2. The CPM myth (and why view-based rates are misleading)

CPM (cost per thousand views) is the metric brands use internally. A $5,000 sponsorship for a video averaging 100k views is a $50 CPM.

The problem is that CPM varies enormously:

- Finance and tech niches: $50–150 CPM. Their audiences have high purchase intent — they're looking to spend money.
- Gaming: $15–40 CPM. Great reach, lower buyer intent for most products.
- Beauty and lifestyle: $25–60 CPM. High purchase intent but very crowded supply.

So when someone tells you "charge $20 CPM," that's meaningless without the niche context. A $20 CPM rate for a finance creator is a massive undercharge. For a gaming creator promoting a VPN, it might be in range.

More importantly: **CPM is the brand's mental model, not yours.** You should think in absolute dollars, not CPMs. "I charge $4,500 for an integration" is what you say in a negotiation. Brands convert that to their CPM math after you give them the number.

Your job is to know the market rate in dollars. The calculator gives you that.

## 3. Deliverable type matters more than audience size

The single biggest driver of rate differences isn't how big your audience is — it's what you're actually delivering.

Here's the rough multiplier structure (integration = 1.0x baseline):

| Deliverable | Multiplier | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Dedicated video | 2–3x | The entire video is about the brand. They get 100% of your attention and creativity. |
| Integration | 1x | A 60–90 second segment in your regular content. This is the standard. |
| Mention | 0.3–0.4x | Brief callout, link in description. Low effort, lower impact. |
| Story / Reel (ephemeral) | 0.25–0.35x | 24-hour content. Ephemeral = lower perceived value. |
| Feed post (static) | 0.5–0.7x | Permanent but passive. Less engagement than video. |
| Host-read podcast ad | ~1x integration | Strong because of intimacy/trust. Equivalent to a YouTube integration at comparable download levels. |
| Multi-platform package | 1.5–2x single deliverable | Bundled deals. More work, more reach, worth more. |

The implication: if you're charging integration rates for a dedicated video, you're leaving 100–200% on the table. This happens constantly.

Flip side: if a brand asks for a "dedicated video" but the budget is integration-level, you're not wrong to say that — and explain why.

## 4. Exclusivity and usage rights: where the real money is

Two terms most creators undervalue — especially early in their career.

**Exclusivity** means you agree not to work with competing brands in a defined window. Standard brand contracts ask for:

- **No exclusivity**: they don't care if you work with competitors. Standard for most deals.
- **30-day category exclusivity**: you won't promote competing products for 30 days. Add 20%.
- **90-day category exclusivity**: 3-month window. Add 50%.
- **Total exclusivity (all categories)**: this is rare and should cost significantly more — you're blocking your entire pipeline.

The calculator uses these as multipliers because they directly affect your other revenue. If a brand locks you out of their category for 3 months, they need to compensate you for that opportunity cost.

**Usage rights** is bigger.

When a brand pays for "organic rights," your video lives on your channel. They can share it and link to it, but they can't run it as an ad independently.

When they ask for **paid amplification rights**, they can take your content and boost it using their ad account — with your face and handle still in it. This is called "whitelisting" or "dark posting." They're effectively buying a more effective ad because it looks organic. Add 40%+.

When they ask for **perpetual rights**, they own the content forever and can use it however they want — including as a TV commercial years from now. This is worth 2.5x the organic rate or more. Many creators sign perpetual rights clauses without understanding what they've agreed to. The standard language is something like "royalty-free, worldwide license in perpetuity in all media." If you see that, charge accordingly or negotiate it down to a limited time window.

## 5. The Karat data and how to read it

Karat Financial publishes the most-cited creator pricing guide in the space. Their 2024 edition covers YouTube specifically, with medians by subscriber tier and content category.

A few things to understand about any published benchmarks:

**They're medians, not ceilings.** Karat's $4,200 median for a 100k-1M tech creator integration means half of deals in that range are below $4,200. The other half are above it — sometimes significantly. Creators with exceptional engagement or niche audiences that brands pay premium for (B2B software, finance with high HHI audiences) routinely exceed the high end.

**They don't account for your specific audience quality.** 500k subscribers is 500k subscribers in the data. But 500k finance subscribers who are 35–55 year old professionals with high household income are worth more than 500k gaming subscribers in the 13–24 cohort. Brands know this. You can negotiate on this.

**They lag the market by 6–12 months.** By the time a 2024 report publishes, it's capturing data from mostly 2023. Creator market rates — especially for TikTok and Shorts — shift faster than annual reports can track. Use the data as a floor, not a ceiling.

**They don't cover your niche perfectly.** The calculator's `dataConfidence` field tells you when we're interpolating versus using direct data. "Low" confidence means we're triangulating from adjacent data — treat those ranges as starting points, not firm market rates.

## 6. What to do when a brand lowballs you

The right response to a lowball offer is not to fold.

Here's tactical language that works:

> "Thanks for coming to me with this. My rate for this deliverable is $[X]. If that's outside your current budget, I'm open to adjusting scope — a shorter integration or removing exclusivity — rather than the base rate. Let me know what works on your end."

What this does:
- Holds your rate (don't drop the number they see first)
- Offers an alternative path that preserves your unit economics
- Puts the ball back in their court

If they come back and say "we can do $[Y]" (below your floor), you have a choice: walk away or negotiate scope down enough to make $Y acceptable. But never drop below your walk-away floor. If the calculator says your floor is $3,500, don't accept $2,000 because "it's a big brand." Big brands have big budgets. They're testing you.

One more thing: if a brand ghosted you after you sent a rate, that's a signal — not a rejection you should lower your rate from. They may have used up their Q budget. They may have gone a different direction. Email them back in 30 days with the same rate.

## 7. What to do when a brand offers more than market

This happens more than creators think, and the default reaction (sign immediately) is often wrong.

If a brand offers significantly above market without you negotiating up, there's usually a reason:

1. **They need perpetual/whitelisting rights and haven't told you yet.** You'll find out when the contract arrives. Read it carefully before countersigning.
2. **There's a tight exclusivity window.** A 90-day category exclusivity can cost you 2-5 other deals. Make sure the math still works.
3. **They want to negotiate the deliverable scope up.** "We have $8k for this" can become a dedicated video + 2 Reels + story + email mention by the time the contract is drafted.
4. **They're testing your floor.** Some brands deliberately offer above market to see if you'll accept more scope for "free" in the negotiation.

None of this means turn it down. It means read the contract before celebrating.

If the rate genuinely reflects market premium without hidden strings — you have leverage. Document it. Use it in future negotiations: "my most recent comparable deal was at $X."
