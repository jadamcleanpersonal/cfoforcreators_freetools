You probably heard "S-corp" from another creator, or your accountant, or some random TikTok. Everyone says it'll save you money on taxes. Most of them are right. But none of them tell you what you're giving up.

This is the honest version.

---

## 1. The 30-second answer (what an S-corp actually IS)

An S-corp is not a company type. It's a **tax election**.

When you form an LLC, the IRS by default taxes you as a sole proprietor — every dollar of profit flows straight to your personal return. S-corp is a form you file (Form 2553) that says "hey IRS, treat this LLC as an S-corp instead." Your LLC doesn't change. Its legal name doesn't change. You just get a different tax structure.

**S-corp is a deal you make with the IRS:**

You agree to run a more formal business — paying yourself a real salary, doing payroll, keeping cleaner books. In exchange, you skip a chunk of self-employment tax on the rest of your income.

If the tax savings are bigger than the extra hassle and cost, you switch. If they're not, you don't.

For most creators, that breakeven point is somewhere around **$60–80k of profit per year** (income minus business expenses). Below that, the formality costs eat the savings. Above that, the savings start to feel real.

---

## 2. The math in plain English: salary vs. distributions

When you're self-employed (just you, or a one-person LLC), every dollar of profit gets hit with an **extra 15.3% self-employment tax** on top of regular income tax. This is Social Security and Medicare — normally your employer pays half and you pay half. When you're self-employed, there's no employer. You owe both halves yourself. On $100k of profit that's an extra $15,300.

When you switch to S-corp, you split your income into two buckets:

- **Salary** — you pay yourself like an employee. The 15.3% SE tax still applies to this bucket.
- **Distributions** — the rest of your profit, paid out separately. This bucket *skips* the 15.3% tax entirely.

That's it. That's the whole game.

**Example:** profit $100k, you pay yourself $55k salary, the other $45k comes out as distributions. That $45k skips the 15.3% tax = **~$6,900 saved**.

## 4. The running costs: what you actually give up

Most "S-corp saves you money!" content stops at the savings. Here's what it leaves out.

### Payroll service
You're now an employee of your own business. That means running payroll for yourself — weekly, bi-weekly, or monthly. A payroll service (Gusto, OnPay, ADP) runs **$30–60/month**, or ~$600–$900/year. It handles withholding, deposits, quarterly forms, and your year-end W-2.

### Higher accountant fees
S-corp tax filing is more complex than a Schedule C. Most accountants charge:
- Sole prop / SMLLC: $300–600/year
- S-corp: $1,000–2,000/year

Annual extra accounting cost: probably **$700–$1,500** more than what you're paying now.

### State filing fees
Varies by state. Most states charge $75–300/year for S-corp registration. California and New Jersey are notable exceptions — see the state gotchas section below.

**The all-in running cost: $1,500–$2,500/year for most creators.**

That means your gross savings need to exceed $1,500–$2,500 just to break even. At $60k profit, the math is tight. At $100k+ profit, it usually works comfortably.

## 5. Reasonable salary: the IRS hot button

The IRS doesn't want you to pay yourself $1 and call everything else a distribution. They expect your salary to be "reasonable" — what you'd have to pay someone else to do your job.

There's no published number. It's judgment. Most creators land somewhere between $40–90k depending on:

- **Your niche**: finance and tech creators face higher salary expectations than gaming or lifestyle creators. The IRS compares you to industry equivalents — a finance creator gets compared to financial analysts ($80–100k market rate), while a gaming streamer might be compared to a media producer ($55–75k).
- **Hours per week**: 40 hrs/week supports a higher salary than 10 hrs/week.
- **Audience size**: at 1M+ subscribers, it's harder to defend a $35k salary.
- **Your state's cost of labor**: $50k in Mississippi and $50k in San Francisco are different things.

**Set the salary too high**: you reduce your tax savings (more goes to the salary bucket).

**Set the salary too low**: audit risk. The IRS knows the "tiny salary, huge distributions" playbook and they look for it. The penalty for getting caught isn't just back taxes — it's also interest and potentially penalties.

Your accountant picks the number with you. Whatever they pick, they should be willing to defend it if asked.

## 6. The 5-year lockout: why this isn't a "try it for a year" decision

This is the one everyone glosses over.

Once you elect S-corp with the IRS, **switching back requires IRS consent**. And once you revoke (either voluntarily or if the IRS terminates it), you can't re-elect for 5 years — unless you get IRS consent for that too.

This matters because:
- If your income drops back below the breakeven point, you're still running payroll. You're stuck paying the overhead on a structure that's no longer saving you money.
- If your situation changes — you bring in a partner, you go back to a day job for a year, your income becomes wildly volatile — you can't just "undo" the election.

**The rule of thumb:** only elect S-corp if you're confident the income level is stable. "I had one great year" is not the same as "I reliably earn above the breakeven." Newer creators (<1 year full-time) almost never have enough income history to justify the commitment.

## 7. State-specific landmines

Six states have S-corp rules that materially affect the math. If you're in one of these, the national "just switch at $60k" rule doesn't apply to you.

### California
California charges S-corps **$800/year minimum franchise tax** plus a **1.5% tax on net S-corp income**. At $70k profit, that's $800 + $1,050 = $1,850 extra per year, on top of standard running costs.

The California math often doesn't work below $80k profit. Don't use the $60k national rule in CA — run your specific numbers.

### New York City
New York state is fine. But if you live within NYC city limits, S-corps owe the **General Corporation Tax (GCT)** at up to 8.85% of net income. This can turn what looks like a good deal into a wash. If you're in NYC, talk to a CPA who knows the city tax.

### New Jersey
New Jersey has a **minimum annual tax** on S-corps ranging from $375 (under $100k gross receipts) to higher. There's also a corporate business tax. Factor these in before switching.

### Oregon
Oregon charges a **$150 minimum excise tax** for most S-corps. There's also a Corporate Activity Tax (CAT) for revenue over $1M — unlikely to affect most creators, but worth knowing.

### Tennessee
Tennessee has no personal income tax (which is great). But S-corps still owe the state's **franchise tax** (minimum $100) and **excise tax** (6.5% on corporate net income). At $100k profit with $60k salary, that's ~$2,600 in TN excise tax. This partially offsets the SE tax savings — the math still usually works but the savings are smaller than in a no-tax state.

### New Hampshire
New Hampshire also has no personal income tax. But S-corps face the **Business Profits Tax** (7.6%) and **Business Enterprise Tax** (0.55%). On $100k of profit, that's meaningful. Run the numbers specifically.

## 8. The "no" case: when you shouldn't switch

Not every creator should elect S-corp. Here's when the honest answer is "don't do it yet":

**Hard no — don't switch:**
- Profit is below ~$60k. The running costs eat the savings.
- You're in California and profit is below ~$80k.
- You've been full-time less than 1 year. The 5-year lockout is too risky with unproven income.
- You're already an S-corp. (If you're wondering whether to revoke, talk to a CPA first.)

**Wait — not yet:**
- Profit is in the $60–80k range. The savings are real but borderline. One bad quarter could flip the math. Wait for one more stable quarter.
- You've been full-time 1–3 years and profit is under $90k. Your income history is still being established.

**Maybe — run the actual numbers:**
- You're above $80k profit but in a high-cost state (CA, NJ, NY).
- Income is growing fast — you might clear the threshold soon.
- You have a W-2 job alongside creator income, which complicates the SE tax picture.

## 9. Filing deadline + what to do once you decide

**The deadline to file Form 2553 is March 15** of the tax year you want the election to apply. Miss it, and you're waiting until next year (barring IRS relief procedures).

If you're reading this after March 15: the IRS does have a late filing relief procedure for businesses that intended to file but missed the deadline. An accountant can help you apply. It's not guaranteed, but it often works if you have a reasonable explanation.

**Step-by-step if you decide to switch:**

1. **Run this calculator** to confirm the math makes sense for your specific situation.
2. **Find an accountant** who has experience with S-corps and creator/freelancer businesses. Ask specifically: "Do you work with content creators?" If they don't know what AdSense revenue is, find someone who does.
3. **Have your accountant file Form 2553** — this is a 2-page form that notifies the IRS of your S-corp election. Your accountant submits it.
4. **Set up payroll** — your accountant or payroll service (Gusto, OnPay) handles this. You'll pick a pay frequency (monthly is common for creators) and a salary amount.
5. **Keep the salary consistent** — don't change it every month. Consistency is part of what makes the salary "defensible" to the IRS.
6. **Come back and re-run the calculator annually** — as income grows, the reasonable salary needs to grow too.
