## 1. Why every clause exists (the brand's lawyer wrote this)

Brand deal contracts aren't written by the marketing manager who emailed you. They're written by a lawyer whose job is to protect the brand from everything that could go wrong. That's not malicious — it's just how legal documents work.

What that means for you: the first draft of every contract will favor the brand. That's not a red flag. That's the starting position.

Your job isn't to accept it or reject it wholesale. Your job is to read it carefully, identify what's unfavorable, and negotiate. Most clauses that look scary are either standard (and fine to sign) or fixable with a counteroffer.

The ones you actually need to worry about are the three or four clauses that can genuinely cost you money, limit your future income, or create legal exposure. That's what this guide is about.

## 2. The five clauses you should never sign without negotiating

Not all clauses are equal. These five show up in bad contracts regularly, and they each have real financial consequences:

**1. Perpetual rights without compensation**
"Creator grants Brand a perpetual, irrevocable, worldwide license to reproduce the Content..."

This means they can use your video forever, in any context, for any purpose, without paying you again. Standard deals grant 6-12 months of organic usage rights. Perpetual rights are worth 2-3x your base rate — if you're not charging for them, you're giving them away.

**2. Overbroad exclusivity**
"Creator agrees not to promote any brand in the [category] industry for [X] months..."

Exclusivity means you can't take competing deals during the window. That's normal and often fair — if they're paying for it. The problem is when the category is too broad (entire "beauty industry" vs. just "skincare"), the duration is too long (18 months for a single video), or the rate doesn't reflect the opportunity cost.

**3. One-sided indemnification**
"Creator shall indemnify and hold harmless Brand against all claims..."

You're taking on liability for anything that comes from the content, but the brand has zero reciprocal obligation. Standard contracts have mutual indemnification — each party covers claims from their own actions.

**4. IP assignment (not a license)**
"Creator assigns to Brand all right, title, and interest in and to the Content..."

"Assigns" is different from "licenses." A license lets them use it under specific conditions. An assignment means you no longer own it. They can modify it, license it to others, or sell it without telling you. This should almost always be a license, not an assignment.

**5. No kill fee**
"Brand may cancel this Agreement at any time for any reason. No compensation shall be owed for work not yet delivered."

If they cancel after you've started creating — after you've done research, scripted, filmed, edited — you get nothing. A kill fee protects against this. Ask for 50% if cancelled after brief approval, 100% if cancelled after first draft delivery.

## 3. Usage rights: the hidden 3x rate multiplier

This is the most underpriced thing in creator contracts.

When a brand can run your video as a paid ad — boosting it, running it as a pre-roll, using it in their own channels — that's called "paid amplification" or "paid usage rights." It's different from organic posting (just posting the video to your channel and letting it sit there).

**Why this matters:**
The production value and creator trust that makes your content valuable to your audience is exactly what makes it valuable as an ad. Brands know this. That's why they want the rights.

**Standard tiers:**
- Organic only (no paid ads): base rate
- Paid usage for 6 months: 1.5-2x base rate
- Paid usage for 12 months: 2-2.5x base rate
- Perpetual paid usage: 2.5-3x+ base rate

**What to look for:**
Any clause mentioning "paid media," "sponsored posts," "dark posts," "whitelisting," "amplification," or "brand boost" means they're paying to run your content as an ad. That usage should be priced accordingly.

If the contract says "organic only" and you see this language, ask what it means specifically. If it's a paid usage right, renegotiate before signing.

## 4. Exclusivity: the right way to scope it

Exclusivity is a real thing brands pay for. If you're promoting their skincare brand, they don't want you also promoting a competitor the same month. That's reasonable.

The problem is when exclusivity clauses are written too broadly. Here's how to scope them correctly:

**Duration:** 30-90 days is standard for a single integration. 6+ months is unusual and worth extra compensation. 12+ months is effectively a retainer deal and should be priced as one.

**Category scope:** "No competing brands in the [specific product] category" is fair. "No brands in the [entire industry]" is not. A gaming mouse deal shouldn't block you from every gaming software deal for a year.

**Platform scope:** Exclusivity should cover the platform the content is on, not your entire creator business. A YouTube integration shouldn't automatically exclude your TikTok.

**What to do:** If the exclusivity is broad, either narrow the scope in a counteroffer or add an exclusivity premium to the rate. The formula most creators use: 15-25% of the base rate per category of exclusivity, per 30-day window.

## 5. Payment terms: Net 30 vs Net 60 vs the gigwage trap

"Net-30" means they pay within 30 days of your invoice. "Net-60" means 60 days. "Net-90" is 90 days.

**The standard is Net-30.** If you're a new creator, you might accept Net-45 from a large brand to build the relationship. Net-60 and beyond is where you should push back.

**The gigwage trap:**
Some contracts say payment is due "within X days of brand approval." This is worse than it looks. The brand controls the approval process. They can delay approving your content indefinitely, which delays your payment clock from starting. Always push for payment terms that begin from your invoice date, not from their approval.

**What to add:**
Even if you accept Net-30, add a late payment clause: "Amounts unpaid after 30 days accrue interest at 1.5% per month." This is standard in most B2B contracts and most brands accept it without pushback. More importantly, it creates an incentive for them to pay on time.

## 6. Kill fees: the safety net most creators forget

Most creators don't negotiate kill fees until they get burned once.

Here's how it works: You agree to a deal. You do the research. You script it. You film it. Maybe you edit it. Then the brand decides they're going in a different direction. Without a kill fee, you just worked for free.

**Standard kill fee structure:**
- Cancelled before content creation begins: 0% (no work done)
- Cancelled after brief approval but before filming: 25-50%
- Cancelled after filming: 50-75%
- Cancelled after first edit delivery: 100%

Some creators also add a "change of scope" clause — if the brand substantially changes the creative brief after you've started, that's treated the same as a cancellation.

**Most brands will accept this.** It's a standard B2B protection. If they push back on a kill fee, that's actually useful information about how they operate.

## 7. Indemnification: the clause that should be mutual

Indemnification clauses determine who's responsible if something goes wrong after the content is published.

You're responsible for things like: your content making a false factual claim, you using copyrighted music without a license, you saying something defamatory about a third party.

The brand is responsible for things like: their product claims being wrong, their brief asking you to say something that isn't true, their use of your content in a context that creates liability.

**The problem:** Many contracts have one-sided indemnification — you cover all claims arising from the content, full stop. The brand has zero reciprocal obligation. This means if they direct you to make a product claim that turns out to be false, you're still on the hook.

**What to ask for:** Mutual indemnification. Each party indemnifies the other for claims arising from their own actions and representations. This is standard in any well-drafted commercial agreement.

## 8. When to call a lawyer

This guide covers how to read and negotiate most creator contracts. Most deals you'll encounter are straightforward enough to handle yourself with the right knowledge.

**Call a lawyer when:**
- The deal is over $25,000+ (the cost of an hour of attorney time is worth it)
- There's an IP assignment (not a license)
- There's a non-compete beyond 6 months or beyond your primary content niche
- There's a governing law clause in a state you don't understand or a country outside the US
- You're signing with a major brand or agency and the contract is 15+ pages
- Something feels off and you can't figure out why

Ask other creators in your niche for referrals. A lawyer who works with creators specifically will be faster and cheaper than a generalist because they've seen these contracts dozens of times.

## The short version

Every brand deal contract starts in the brand's favor. That's normal. Your job is to read it, find the three or four things worth negotiating, and make those asks. Most brands expect some negotiation and will accommodate reasonable requests.

The four most important things:
1. Know your usage rights tiers and price them accordingly
2. Scope exclusivity correctly (duration + category + platform)
3. Protect yourself with a kill fee
4. Ask for mutual indemnification

Everything else is usually fine to sign, or fine to let go in exchange for something more important.
