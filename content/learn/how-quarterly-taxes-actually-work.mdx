## the basics: what quarterly taxes are and why they exist

the US tax system runs on pay-as-you-go. the government wants your money throughout the year, not as a lump sum in april.

if you work a W-2 job, your employer handles this automatically — they withhold federal and state tax from every paycheck. you probably never think about it.

as a creator, you're your own employer. no one withholds anything. the IRS expects you to send in payments yourself, four times a year, to cover your taxes as you earn.

if you don't, two things happen:
1. you face an underpayment penalty (we'll get to how big it is in a minute — spoiler: not that big)
2. you owe a massive bill in april that you may not have the cash for

the quarterly system is mostly about cash flow management. it forces you to keep money aside as you earn it. creators who ignore quarterly taxes often end up in a cycle of April scrambles, payment plans, and stress. you don't want that.

## the four deadlines (yes, the Q2 gap is intentional)

here's where people get tripped up. the deadlines do not follow a neat three-month pattern.

| quarter | income period | payment due |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| Q1 | January 1 – March 31 | **April 15** |
| Q2 | April 1 – May 31 | **June 15** |
| Q3 | June 1 – August 31 | **September 15** |
| Q4 | September 1 – December 31 | **January 15** (following year) |

notice Q2 covers only two months (April–May) but has three months until the deadline. and Q3 covers three months but the deadline comes fast. this is intentional IRS calendar math — they're trying to avoid conflicts with the April filing deadline. don't try to logic your way through it. just memorize the dates or put them in your calendar: **April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15**.

if a deadline falls on a weekend or federal holiday, it shifts to the next business day. the IRS publishes the exact dates each year.

## how safe harbor works (and why it's your friend)

"safe harbor" is the IRS rule that protects you from underpayment penalties, even if you end up owing more at filing.

the rule: pay at least 90% of your current-year tax liability throughout the year, and you're safe. (there's also a method based on last year's tax — 100% of last year's bill, or 110% if your prior-year AGI was over $150k. whichever is less. for most creators, the 90% of current year method works fine.)

why does this matter? your creator income is variable. if your channel blows up in Q4, your tax liability shoots up. if you paid even amounts throughout the year based on earlier estimates, you might owe more in april — but you won't be penalized as long as you hit 90% of the final number.

practical application: estimate your annual income, calculate your full-year tax, take 90% of that, divide by 4, and pay that each quarter. if your income was higher than expected, you'll owe a small true-up in April. if lower, you'll get a refund or credit.

our calculator uses the 90%-of-current-year method. if you had significant income last year and your income dropped, talk to a CPA — the prior-year method might save you cash.

## SE tax — the thing W-2 employees never see

here's the brutal surprise most creators hit in their first year: **self-employment tax**.

when you work for a company, your employer pays 7.65% of your wages into Social Security and Medicare. you pay another 7.65%. total: 15.3%.

as a creator, you're the employer and the employee. you pay both halves. that's 15.3% on your net self-employment income, right off the top. before federal income tax.

the good news:
- you can deduct half of SE tax from your income before calculating income tax
- the 20% QBI deduction (for sole proprietors) can cut your taxable income significantly (more on this below)
- SE tax only applies to net income — gross income minus your business expenses

the SS portion of SE tax has a wage base cap ($176,100 in 2025). above that income, you stop paying the 12.4% Social Security portion and only pay the 2.9% Medicare piece. if you also have a W-2 job, your W-2 wages count toward that cap, reducing your SE Social Security liability.

rough math: if you make $100k net as a creator, expect about $14,130 in SE tax alone, before income tax.

## the QBI deduction — 20% off your taxable income

if you're a sole proprietor (which most creators are), you qualify for the Section 199A deduction: 20% of your qualified business income (net SE income). this directly reduces your taxable income.

example: $100k net creator income. QBI deduction = $20k. you only pay income tax on $80k instead of $100k.

there's an income phase-out starting at $197,300 (single, 2025) and $394,600 (married filing jointly). if your taxable income is above those thresholds, the deduction phases out — and disappears entirely once you hit $247,300 single / $494,600 MFJ, because at that level the rules around W-2 wages and qualified property apply (and sole props typically have neither). if you're in that range, talk to a CPA about whether an S-corp makes sense for you.

## state tax — wildly different state by state

nine states have no income tax on earned wages: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire (on wages), South Dakota, Tennessee, Washington, Wyoming, and Texas. if you're in one of these, you only owe federal quarterly payments.

nine states have a flat rate (a single % on all income): Arizona (2.5%), Colorado (4.4%), Illinois (4.95%), Indiana (3.05%), Kentucky (4.0%), Massachusetts (5.0%), Michigan (4.25%), Pennsylvania (3.07%), and Utah (4.65%).

the rest use brackets — like federal taxes, but with their own rates and thresholds. California is the most punishing (up to 13.3%). New York, New Jersey, and Oregon also run high. Most other bracket states are in the 4–7% range.

state standard deductions vary wildly and are often much lower than the federal $15,000. California's is only $5,202.

most states require you to make quarterly estimated payments separately from your federal payment. you don't send state money to the IRS — you send it to your state's department of revenue.

our calculator gives you a state estimate, but state tax laws change frequently (several states have cut rates in 2023–2025). confirm your rate at your state's revenue department website or with an accountant before filing.

## the penalty for missing a payment (small, not catastrophic)

the underpayment penalty is calculated like interest on the amount you should have paid. the rate is the federal short-term rate plus 3% — in 2024–2025, that's been around 8% annualized.

if you missed a $3,000 Q1 payment and realized it at Q3 deadline (~5 months late):
`$3,000 × 8% × (5/12) = ~$100`

that's roughly $100. annoying, not life-ruining.

the penalty isn't a fixed fine. it's proportional to how much you underpaid and for how long. if your income was genuinely lower than expected, the penalty may be zero (because your actual tax liability is lower too).

two things you CAN'T do with the penalty:
- you can't make it disappear by paying late — the interest has already accrued
- you can't get a waiver just by asking nicely (though there are formal waiver procedures for first-time cases)

an Enrolled Agent can formally dispute an underpayment penalty if you have grounds (significant income fluctuation, natural disaster, etc.).

## what to do right now if you're already behind

if you've missed Q1 or Q2 payments, here's what to do:

1. **don't panic.** the penalty is small. calculate it so you know what you're dealing with.
2. **send a catch-up payment immediately.** the underpayment clock stops when you pay. use [IRS Direct Pay](https://directpay.irs.gov) — it takes 3 minutes and there's no fee.
3. **on your payment, select the right tax year and payment type** (1040-ES quarterly estimated tax). don't accidentally apply it to last year.
4. **stay current for the remaining quarters.** missing the next deadline compounds the problem.
5. **file Form 2210 with your tax return** if you want to formally calculate the exact penalty (your tax software usually does this automatically).

if you're significantly behind and owe a large catch-up payment that you can't make in full, talk to an Enrolled Agent. there are installment options and, in some cases, penalty abatement for first-time situations.

## frequently asked questions

**do I have to make quarterly payments if I made no money yet?**
no. if your estimated tax liability for the year is less than $1,000, you don't need to pay quarterly. but once you cross that threshold, start paying.

**what if my income is completely unpredictable (e.g., viral video income)?**
estimate conservatively. if you underpay and owe a penalty, it's small. if you overpay, you get a refund. the pain of underpayment is always worse than overpayment.

**can I just pay it all in April?**
technically yes, but you'll owe the underpayment penalty for each quarter you missed. for most creators, it's better to pay quarterly and avoid the lump-sum shock.

**what's the difference between IRS Direct Pay and EFTPS?**
IRS Direct Pay is easiest for one-off payments — no account required, just bank details and a few clicks. EFTPS (Electronic Federal Tax Payment System) is better if you make many payments throughout the year — it has a scheduling feature. Both are free.

**do I owe quarterly taxes on money from all platforms?**
yes. AdSense, brand deals, Patreon, affiliate commissions, merch, courses — all of it is self-employment income and all of it is subject to both SE tax and income tax.

**what about money held in PayPal or Stripe — do I owe quarterly taxes on it even if I haven't transferred to my bank?**
yes. income is recognized when you receive it (constructive receipt), not when you transfer it. if it's in your PayPal account and available to you, it's income for that quarter.
