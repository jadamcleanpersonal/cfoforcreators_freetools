# Should You Form an LLC? Here's the Real Answer.

---

You probably heard "you should form an LLC" from another creator, or your accountant, or a YouTube video. They were probably right, but they probably didn't tell you why it's actually useful, what it costs, or when it's a waste of money.

This is the honest version.

---

## The 30-second answer

An LLC ("Limited Liability Company") is a way to legally separate "you" from "your business." If your channel ever gets sued, the lawsuit goes after the business — not your personal house, savings, or car.

For most creators:
- **Under $1,000/month:** wait. Don't bother yet.
- **$1,000–$5,000/month:** optional. Useful if you have brand deals, merch, or in-person fans.
- **$5,000+/month consistent:** yes, basically always worth it.

Cost to set up: $50–500 one-time, depending on your state.
Cost to keep it running: $0–800/year, also depending on state.
Time to set up: 1–2 hours of paperwork, filed in a week.

**One huge misconception to clear up before you read further:** an LLC by itself does **NOT save you money on taxes**. The IRS taxes a one-person LLC exactly the same as a regular self-employed person. The tax savings come later, when you ask the IRS to treat your LLC as an S-corp on top — that's a separate decision.

---

## What you actually get

### 1. A wall between you and your business

This is the whole reason LLCs exist. Without one:

- A brand sues you over a video → they can come after everything you own.
- A viewer trips on your camera at a meet-and-greet → they can sue **you** personally.
- An editor sues you over unpaid work → your house is on the table.

With an LLC:

- Lawsuits hit the business, not you personally.
- Your personal stuff (house, savings, car, retirement accounts) stays protected.
- Worst case the business goes bankrupt — but you, personally, don't.

One catch worth knowing: courts can "pierce the veil" (their words) if you mix personal and business money together. So the LLC only protects you if you treat it like a real separate thing — separate bank account, no using the business card for personal stuff, etc. Mess that up and a judge can decide the LLC is fake and let the lawsuit through.

### 2. A clean business name on contracts

Instead of brands writing checks to "[Your Name]," they write them to "[Your Channel] LLC." Three small wins from this:

- Looks more professional in brand contracts.
- Some brands actually require you to have a registered business before they'll work with you.
- Your real name doesn't have to appear on every legal document floating around the internet.

### 3. A business tax ID (called an EIN)

Once you form the LLC, you get an EIN (think of it as a Social Security Number for your business). You give it to brands and payment platforms instead of your real SSN. That's a meaningful privacy and identity-theft win — your SSN doesn't get passed around to every brand's bookkeeper.

It's free, takes 10 minutes online at IRS.gov.

### 4. The path to tax savings later

You can't switch to S-corp tax treatment (the big creator tax savings move) without first being an LLC or a corporation. So forming an LLC opens the door to bigger savings later, even if you don't do anything special with taxes today.

## What you give up

Short list. LLCs are pretty low-friction compared to S-corps.

### 1. A one-time setup cost

You pay your state a filing fee to register the LLC. Range:
- Cheapest states (~$50): Arizona, Kentucky, Mississippi, Missouri
- Average ($100–200): most states
- Most expensive: Massachusetts ($500), Tennessee ($300+)

Add another $0–300 if you use a service (LegalZoom, Bizee, your state's online portal) to handle the paperwork for you. You can do it yourself for just the state fee if you don't mind reading a few forms.

### 2. Annual fees in some states

Most states charge a yearly fee to keep the LLC active. The big ones to know about:
- **California: $800/year minimum.** Even if you make $0. This is the brutal one.
- **New York:** $25–$4,500/year depending on revenue, plus a one-time "publication requirement" that can cost $1,000+ if you live in NYC.
- **Massachusetts:** $500/year.
- **Delaware:** $300/year.
- **Most other states:** $0–$200/year.

If you live in California or NYC, the annual cost is the biggest reason to wait until you're making enough income to justify it.

### 3. A bit more paperwork

You need to:
- File an annual report in some states (usually a 5-minute online form).
- Keep business and personal money completely separate. No buying groceries on the business card. Ever.
- Sign things as the LLC, not as yourself personally.

This isn't hard. It's just one more thing to remember.

### 4. It doesn't save you money on taxes

This is the most misunderstood thing about LLCs. An LLC by itself does **not** lower your tax bill. The IRS treats a one-person LLC exactly like a regular self-employed person — same forms, same taxes, same everything.

The tax savings come later, when you elect S-corp on top of the LLC. The LLC alone is just legal protection.

If someone is telling you "form an LLC to save on taxes" — they're either confused or selling you something.

## When does it actually make sense?

**Probably YES:**
- Making $5k+/month consistently.
- Signing brand contracts where someone could potentially come after you legally.
- Selling any kind of product (merch, courses, services).
- Meeting fans in person at events or shoots.
- Hiring contractors (editors, thumbnail designers, managers).
- Live in a state with low LLC fees ($200/year or less).

**Probably NO (or wait):**
- Under $1,000/month and not growing quickly.
- Live in California or NYC and haven't crossed ~$3,000/month consistent.
- You're brand new, no contracts yet, no merch, no in-person events.
- You're not sure the channel will still be your main thing in 12 months.

**Maybe — depends on your situation:**
- $1,000–$5,000/month.
- Just started getting brand deals.
- Audience growing fast and likely to keep growing.

## What changes in your day-to-day

Five practical things change after you form an LLC:

1. **You open a separate business checking account** under the LLC name, using the EIN. All creator income flows in here. All creator expenses come out of here.

2. **Brands ask for a W-9 with the LLC name + EIN** instead of your name + SSN. Less paperwork floating around with your personal info.

3. **You sign things as the LLC** — "Jada's Channel LLC, by Jada Smith, Owner" instead of just "Jada Smith." Feels weird the first few times.

4. **You don't pay yourself a salary.** Money in the LLC account is your money. Transfer to personal whenever. (This changes if you later elect S-corp — then you pay yourself a real salary.)

5. **Tax filing stays the same.** A one-person LLC files the exact same tax forms as a regular self-employed person. No new complexity until you elect S-corp.

## Common mistakes

The ones that actually bite people:

1. **Mixing personal and business money.** Paying for groceries with the business card. Paying your kid's daycare out of the business account. Move money back and forth casually. This is the #1 way LLCs get "pierced" in court — the judge looks at your bank records and says "this isn't a real business, it's just an account." Then the lawsuit gets to your personal stuff anyway.

2. **Forming in Delaware/Nevada/Wyoming when you live somewhere else.** Some YouTube video told you "form in Delaware for tax benefits." That's actually for big corporations, not LLCs. As a creator working from your home, you usually have to register where you actually live and work — which means forming elsewhere ends up costing you DOUBLE state filing fees.

3. **Forgetting to file the annual report.** Some states will dissolve your LLC if you miss a year. Set a calendar reminder.

4. **Not getting business insurance.** The LLC protects you from losing personal assets in a lawsuit, but it doesn't pay the legal bills. Get a general liability policy. Usually $200–500/year. Worth it.

5. **Thinking the LLC saves money on taxes.** It doesn't. The LLC is for protection. Tax savings come from electing S-corp on top.

## What to do next

1. **Run the calculator** to confirm you're in the income range where it's worth it.
2. **Pick your state.** Almost always your home state. Don't overthink it.
3. **File the paperwork.** Either do it yourself on your state's website ($50–500 in state fees) or use LegalZoom / Bizee / Northwest Registered Agent ($100–300 on top for hand-holding).
4. **Get an EIN from the IRS.** Free, 10 minutes at IRS.gov.
5. **Open a business checking account** in the LLC name (Karat, Found, Lili, Mercury, or your existing bank).
6. **Update all your accounts** — AdSense, sponsors, Stripe, PayPal — to pay the LLC instead of you personally.

That's it. The whole thing takes about a week of small tasks.
