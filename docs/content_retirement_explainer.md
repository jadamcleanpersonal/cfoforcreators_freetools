There's no employer matching your 401(k). No HR person enrolling you on day one. Just you, your unpredictable income, and a stack of confusing retirement account names — Roth IRA, Solo 401(k), SEP-IRA, Traditional IRA — that nobody ever explained.

Here's the plain-English version, with a clear answer for your situation.

---

## The 30-second answer

For most creators, the right choice comes down to how much you can save each year and whether you might ever hire someone:

- **Saving small ($50–500/month)?** → **Roth IRA.** Simple. Tax-free in retirement.
- **Saving real money ($500–6,000/month)?** → **Solo 401(k).** Highest limits, plus a Roth option.
- **Want simple AND high limits, no plans to hire?** → **SEP-IRA.** Easier than Solo 401(k) but with one catch (see below).
- **In a really high tax bracket and want the deduction now?** → **Traditional IRA** (limited use case).

All four are easy to open at Fidelity, Schwab, or Vanguard. All four are free. None of them require a financial advisor.

The biggest mistake creators make is **not starting at all**. The second biggest is starting too small. Almost everything else is fixable.

## What each account actually is (in plain English)

### Roth IRA

The simplest one. You put in money you've **already paid tax on**, and when you pull it out in retirement, you owe **zero tax** — including on all the growth.

- 2026 limit: $7,500/year ($8,600 if you're 50+).
- Opens in 10 minutes at any brokerage.
- Cap: if you make more than $161,000 single / $240,000 married, you can't put money in directly. (There are workarounds — your accountant calls them "backdoor Roths" — but get help with those.)
- Bonus: you can pull out what you contributed (not the growth) anytime without penalty. So if you ever desperately need cash, your past contributions can come back out.

### Solo 401(k)

The biggest savings vehicle for one-person businesses. The way it works: the IRS lets you contribute money two ways — as yourself (the "employee") AND as your business (the "employer") — into the same account.

- 2026 limit: up to ~$70,000/year combined. Yes, really.
  - As the employee: up to $23,500/year.
  - As the employer: 25% of your business profit, on top.
- Has both regular AND Roth options. (The Roth option for high contribution amounts is one of the strongest reasons to pick this over SEP-IRA.)
- You can borrow from it in a real emergency (up to $50,000, has to be paid back).
- **Important:** has to be opened by December 31 of the year you want to use it for. Miss that date and you lose a full year.
- Available at Fidelity, Schwab, E-Trade. Opens in 1–2 weeks (more paperwork than a Roth IRA).
- Slightly more paperwork once your balance crosses $250,000 (one extra IRS form per year — your accountant does it).

### SEP-IRA

"Simplified" is in the name. It's basically a 401(k) with way less paperwork.

- 2026 limit: up to 25% of your business profit, capped at ~$70,000.
- **No Roth option.** Money goes in pre-tax, you owe tax when you pull it out in retirement.
- No paperwork once it's open.
- Available at any brokerage.
- **THE CATCH:** if you ever hire an employee (any employee), the SEP-IRA rules require you to fund their account at the same percentage rate as yours. Permanently. This is the silent trap.
- Can't borrow from it.

### Traditional IRA

The opposite of a Roth. You put in pre-tax money (lowering your tax bill now), and pay tax when you pull it out in retirement.

- 2026 limit: $7,500/year ($8,600 if 50+) — same as Roth.
- Useful in specific situations (you're in a really high tax bracket, you're rolling over an old 401(k) from a previous day job).
- For most creators starting out, Roth is the better choice — your tax bracket is probably lower now than it'll be when you're 60.

## What you get with each

### Roth IRA gives you:
- The simplest account to open
- Tax-free money in retirement
- Your contributions can come back out anytime (the growth can't)
- The ability to start small without paperwork

### Solo 401(k) gives you:
- By far the highest contribution limit
- Both regular AND Roth options
- The ability to borrow from it in a pinch
- The "I'm a serious business" feeling

### SEP-IRA gives you:
- Almost zero paperwork
- High contribution limit
- Quick to open

### Traditional IRA gives you:
- A tax deduction RIGHT NOW (if your income is in the right range)
- Useful for rolling over old 401(k)s

---

## What you give up / take on with each

### Roth IRA downsides:
- Limited contribution — $7,500/year is small if you're a high earner.
- Income cap — if you make above $161k single, you can't contribute directly.
- The growth is locked up until 59½ (the contributions can come out, but the gains can't).

### Solo 401(k) downsides:
- More paperwork to open (compared to a Roth IRA's 10-minute setup).
- Has to be opened by December 31 (Roth IRA gives you until April 15).
- One extra annual IRS form once your balance hits $250k.
- More moving parts if you have multiple income sources.

### SEP-IRA downsides:
- **No Roth option.** This is huge. For most younger creators, Roth treatment is way better than getting the tax deduction now.
- If you ever hire ANY employee, you have to fund their account too, permanently.
- Can't borrow from it.
- No "catch-up" contributions if you're 50+ like a 401(k) has.

### Traditional IRA downsides:
- Same low limit as Roth ($7,500/year).
- You'll owe tax on everything when you pull it out — including all the growth.

## When does each one make sense?

**Pick Roth IRA if:**
- You're just starting to save.
- You can save under ~$7,500/year for now.
- You make under $161k single / $240k married.
- You want the simplest possible option.

**Pick Solo 401(k) if:**
- You're a full-time creator with no employees.
- You can save more than $7,500/year.
- You want the Roth option for higher contribution amounts.
- You're okay with slightly more paperwork.

**Pick SEP-IRA if:**
- You're a full-time creator with no employees AND no plans to hire.
- You want the easiest high-limit option.
- You don't care about Roth treatment.
- You don't need the option to borrow from it.

**Pick Traditional IRA if:**
- You're in a really high tax bracket right now and want the deduction.
- You're rolling over an old 401(k) from a previous job.
- You can't use Roth IRA (income too high) and don't have a Solo 401(k) yet.

## Our honest recommendation for most creators

If we had to pick one without knowing anything else about you, the answer is almost always **Solo 401(k), Roth version.**

Reasons:
1. The contribution limit is 9x higher than a Roth IRA, so you'll never outgrow it.
2. The Roth option means tax-free money in retirement on contributions much bigger than a Roth IRA would allow.
3. The "I might hire later" trap that hurts SEP-IRA users isn't a problem here.
4. The borrowing option is a real safety net for irregular-income people.

The one reason to pick something else: if you're saving less than $7,500/year right now and don't expect that to change soon, Roth IRA is simpler with no real downside at that level.

## What changes in your life

Once you open one (whichever you pick):

1. **You set up automatic contributions.** Pick a percentage of every payment you receive that flows into the account automatically. 10–15% is a good target if you can swing it. Even 5% beats 0%.

2. **The money invests itself.** Pick a target-date fund or a simple index fund. Forget about it. Don't pick individual stocks — your retirement account isn't a place to gamble.

3. **You don't touch it.** That's the whole point. Pretend it's not there.

4. **At tax time:** your contributions either lower your tax bill (traditional / SEP) or come from money you already paid tax on (Roth).

5. **At retirement:** you pull it out, either tax-free (Roth) or taxed at your retirement bracket (traditional).

## Common mistakes

The big ones:

1. **Waiting too long to start.** Compound interest cuts both ways — every year you wait is way more than 1/40th of your retirement money. Even $200/month starting at 25 outperforms $1,000/month starting at 45.

2. **Picking SEP-IRA without thinking about hiring.** If there's any chance you'll formalize a contractor as an employee someday (an editor on payroll, a manager, an assistant), the SEP-IRA "fund theirs too" rule becomes a real problem. Solo 401(k) avoids this entirely.

3. **Maxing Roth IRA when you could do Solo 401(k).** If you can save more than $7,500/year, you're leaving money on the table by only using a Roth IRA.

4. **Missing the December 31 deadline for Solo 401(k).** Roth IRA gives you until April 15 of next year. Solo 401(k) needs to be OPEN by December 31 (you can contribute later, but the account has to exist). Miss it and you lose a full year of contributions.

5. **Trying to time the market or pick stocks.** Don't. Target-date funds or 3-fund portfolios. Boring works.

6. **Contributing inconsistently.** "I'll save when I have a good month" never happens. Set a percentage of every payment and let it run.

7. **Pulling money out early.** Penalties + lost compounding = the most expensive thing you can do.

## What to do next

1. **Open the right account at Fidelity, Schwab, or Vanguard.** All three are free, all three are equally good. Don't agonize over which.
2. **Set up automatic contributions** — pick a percentage of every income hit (5–15%) that routes in.
3. **Pick a target-date fund or a simple index fund.** Done.
4. **Don't look at it for 30 years.**
