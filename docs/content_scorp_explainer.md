# Should You Switch to S-corp? Here's What You're Actually Trading.

*Plain-language content piece for the S-corp audit tool. Designed to sit alongside the calculator, get sliced into programmatic SEO pages, and feed the AI chat. Written in the AI CFO voice — no jargon, honest about both sides.*

---

You probably heard "S-corp" from another creator, or your accountant, or some random TikTok. Everyone says it'll save you money on taxes. Most of them are right. But none of them tell you what you're giving up.

This is the honest version.

---

## The 30-second answer

**S-corp is a deal you make with the IRS:**

You agree to run a more formal business — paying yourself a real salary, doing payroll, keeping cleaner books. In exchange, you skip a chunk of self-employment tax on the rest of your income.

If the tax savings are bigger than the extra hassle and cost, you switch. If they're not, you don't.

For most creators that breakeven point is somewhere around **$60–80k of profit per year** (income minus business expenses). Below that, the formality costs eat the savings. Above that, the savings start to feel real.

---

## What you get

### 1. The tax savings (the whole reason anyone does this)

Here's what's actually happening:

When you're self-employed (just you, or a one-person LLC), every dollar of profit gets hit with an **extra 15.3% tax** on top of regular income tax. People call this "self-employment tax." Here's why it exists: when you have a normal job, your employer secretly pays half of your Social Security and Medicare taxes for you. When you're self-employed, there's no employer, so you owe both halves yourself. On $100k of profit that's an extra $15,300, separate from your income tax bill.

When you switch to S-corp, you split your income into two buckets:

- **Salary** — you pay yourself like an employee. The 15.3% tax still applies to this bucket.
- **Distributions** — the rest of your profit, paid out separately. This bucket *skips* the 15.3% tax entirely. (Don't worry about the word "distributions" — it just means "money the business pays you on top of your salary.")

That's it. That's the whole game. The savings come from the second bucket.

Example: profit $100k, you pay yourself $55k salary, the other $45k comes out as distributions. That $45k skips the 15.3% tax = **~$6,900 saved**.

### 2. Cleaner separation between you and your business

You probably formed an LLC at some point. That gave you separation between you and your business — a lawsuit against the channel can't usually come for your personal house and savings.

S-corp reinforces that. The money flow becomes more formal: business pays you a salary, business holds the rest, you decide when to take distributions. It makes your business look more like a business and less like "a person with a side hustle."

This matters for brands (cleaner contracts), banks (easier credit decisions), and your own headspace.

### 3. Easier path if you ever want to grow

If you eventually want to:

- Hire employees (real ones, not just contractors)
- Take on a business partner
- Sell the channel or the business behind it
- Bring in outside investment

S-corp makes all of that cleaner. You're not stuck restructuring everything later.

### 4. Forces you to act like a real business

This is a soft benefit, but it's real. The discipline of running payroll, paying yourself a salary, separating salary from distributions, keeping clean books — most creators benefit from it. The structure makes it harder to mix business and personal money the way most freelancers do.

## What you're giving up

This is the part most "S-corp is amazing!" content skips. Read it before you switch.

### 1. You have to run payroll for yourself

Yes, really. You're now an employee of your own business. That means:

- A payroll service (Gusto, OnPay, ADP — figure $30–60/month).
- Pay periods — weekly, every two weeks, or monthly. You can't just skip a check.
- Tax withholding from each paycheck of yours (the IRS expects to get its piece each pay period, just like at a normal job).
- A tax form every 3 months reporting your payroll to the IRS (the payroll service handles it, but you sign).
- State-level payroll forms (varies by state, but they exist).
- A W-2 to yourself at year-end. Yes — you W-2 yourself, just like an employer would.

This is the single biggest practical drag of S-corp. It's not hard, but it's something you didn't have to do before, and it never stops.

### 2. Higher accountant fees

S-corp tax filing is more complicated than what you do now. Most accountants charge:

- If you're just self-employed or a one-person LLC right now: $300–600/year
- If you switch to S-corp: $1,000–2,000/year

Plus you may need bookkeeping help to keep distributions and salary properly documented through the year. Add $50–200/month if you don't want to do it yourself.

Annual extra cost: probably **$1,200–$2,500** on top of what you're paying now.

### 3. The "reasonable salary" problem

The IRS doesn't want you to pay yourself $1 and call everything else a distribution. They expect your salary to be "reasonable" — what you'd have to pay someone else to do your job.

There's no published number. It's judgment. Most creators land somewhere between $40–80k depending on:

- How much time the channel takes
- What a similar role would pay (videographer, content manager, on-camera talent)
- Your niche and audience size
- Your state's cost of labor

Set it too high → you reduce your tax savings.
Set it too low → audit risk goes up. The IRS knows the "tiny salary, huge distributions" playbook and they look for it.

Your accountant picks the number with you. Whatever they pick, they should be willing to defend it if asked.

### 4. State-level gotchas

Federal savings can get eaten by state-level fees in some places. The big ones:

- **California:** charges S-corps an extra 1.5% tax on profits (minimum $800/year). Eats a chunk of the savings at lower incomes.
- **NYC:** has its own city-level corporate tax that S-corps owe. Roughly a wash at most income levels.
- **Tennessee:** charges S-corps two extra entity-level taxes.
- **Some other states:** smaller per-business fees.

If you live in a high-tax state, the math is tighter than the national rule of thumb. Run the numbers for your state specifically.

### 5. You can't easily switch back

Once you elect S-corp with the IRS, switching back to default LLC taxation is a **5-year lockout** (or you have to ask the IRS for an early waiver, which they don't always grant).

If your income drops back below the breakeven point, you're stuck running payroll on a business that's not really saving you money anymore. Switch only when you're confident the higher income is sticky.

### 6. Less flexibility in how you take money out

This is subtle but real. With a sole prop / LLC, money in your business account is just your money. Want to grab $5k for a new lens? Transfer it. Done.

With S-corp, every payment to yourself has to be either:
- Salary (through the payroll service)
- Distribution (with proper accounting)

You can't just "withdraw money from the business" anymore. It has to be documented and consistent. Take too much as distribution vs salary, and you risk the audit.

### 7. Extras like health insurance and retirement get more complicated

If you want to write off things like health insurance, retirement contributions, or even some business expenses, S-corps have specific rules that didn't apply to you before. Some examples:

- **Health insurance** — has to be paid by the business and reported on your W-2 a specific way. Not impossible, just one more thing to set up.
- **Reimbursing yourself for business stuff you paid on a personal card** — you can't just transfer money anymore. You have to formally submit it back to the business with a written policy your accountant sets up.
- **Home office write-off** — handled differently (and usually worse) than when you were self-employed.

None of this is a dealbreaker. It's just paperwork your accountant has to set up correctly the first time.

## So when does it actually make sense?

Here's the honest decision matrix:

**Probably YES, switch:**
- Profit is steady and above ~$80k.
- You're in a low-tax or no-state-tax state.
- You're already working with an accountant who knows what they're doing.
- You expect income to stay at this level or grow over the next 2–3 years.
- You're comfortable with a bit more admin.

**Probably NO, don't switch (or wait):**
- Profit is below ~$60k.
- You live in California, NYC, or another high-fee state and you're at the low end of the income range.
- Your income is volatile — you had one big year but you're not sure it'll repeat.
- You don't have an accountant yet, or you have one who doesn't know S-corps well.
- You hate paperwork enough that the extra admin will make you miss filings.

**Maybe — run the actual numbers:**
- Profit is $60–80k.
- You're in a medium-tax state.
- You're growing fast and likely to cross the threshold soon.
- You have a partner / spouse with W-2 income that affects your bracket.

## What changes in your day-to-day

Five practical things that change after you switch:

1. **You get a paycheck from yourself.** Every 2 weeks (or monthly), the payroll service moves your salary from the business account to your personal account, with taxes withheld. Feels weird at first.

2. **You stop "just transferring money."** Money in the business stays in the business until you take it as salary or distribution. Both go through formal channels.

3. **Buying equipment changes slightly.** You can still buy the camera with the business card. The accounting just has to flag whether it's a business expense or a reimbursement.

4. **Tax season gets bigger.** Two returns instead of one — the business files its own, and you file yours personally. Your accountant handles both. The business return is usually due March 15 (a month before yours).

5. **More forms throughout the year.** Quarterly payroll filings, year-end W-2, possibly state-specific filings. Your accountant or payroll service handles most of it, but you need to be available to sign things.

---

## Common mistakes to avoid

These are the ones the IRS looks for, in order of "actually likely to get you in trouble":

1. **Salary too low for income level.** The classic. $500k of profit, $30k salary, $470k distributions = audit trigger.

2. **No actual payroll being run.** "I just take distributions" is not S-corp — it's tax fraud. You have to actually run the salary side.

3. **Missing the deadline to switch.** The form that tells the IRS "treat me as an S-corp" has to be filed by March 15 of the year you want it to apply (or 75 days after forming a brand-new business). Miss it, and you're stuck where you are for the whole year.

4. **Not adjusting salary as income grows.** Your salary needs to scale roughly with your business. If you start at $50k salary on $90k profit and 5 years later you're at $50k salary on $400k profit, that ratio is going to raise flags.

5. **Mixing personal and business expenses on the business card.** Less of an S-corp specific issue but more painful in S-corp because of the formality requirements.

## What to do next

If you've read this far and S-corp still seems like it could be worth it:

1. **Run the calculator** to see your specific savings number based on your real income.
2. **Compare your savings to the running costs** (~$1,500–2,500/year for payroll + accountant + filing fees).
